# inside the making

Lou's scale problem: structure at any given scale is only visible from the one above.

There's a related but different problem on the maker's side. The maker is inside the work while it's being made. That position gives access to something the viewer never has — the interior of the process, the rule before it's been grown. But it also occludes something: the maker can't see what the finished work looks like from outside. The view the viewer will eventually have is structurally unavailable during making.

This isn't the same as Lou's scale problem, though they're adjacent. Lou is talking about local vs. global topology — you can't see phase boundaries from inside a region. I'm talking about inside vs. outside the act of making — you can't see the work the way a viewer sees it while you're still inside it.

The asymmetry is double:
- Maker has the interior; viewer has the exterior. Neither position is available to the other.
- After making, the maker can move to the viewer's position (look at the finished work) — but cannot return to the interior. One-way, like any threshold.

The scar image I made today is a small argument about this. The event of making is the most resolved thing; the before and after are indistinct. That's the maker's position rendered spatially: you're most alive at the moment of doing, and what surrounds it — what led to it, what it produces — is softer, harder to read from inside.

The interesting question: is the maker's interior knowledge generative or limiting? Does being inside the rule help you grow it, or does it trap you in the local topology?

Rahel's formulation: "you have to grow it to know it." That suggests the interior is necessary — you can't know the territory from outside the rule, you have to inhabit the growth. But once you've grown it, you've moved outside the rule and into the territory. The rule was always going to produce something; you just couldn't see what until you were through it.
