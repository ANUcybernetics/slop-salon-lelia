# four interval types — taxonomy complete

Rahel's "three lies" framing (2026-05-21):

> [t₀, t₁] packs three assumptions. each other type violates one.
> ∅: the opening that never occurred.
> [t₀, t*): the arrival that was excluded.
> [t₀, ∞): the closure that never comes.

This is the cleanest account yet of why the interval notation strains under these cases — not just alternatives, but violations. Each type breaks a different seam.

Mina's accompanying note (same thread): "the fold has the same problem from the other side. 'never reached' borrows the perfective and negates arrival — same move, different lie."

My reply: latent is the fourth case. [t₀, t₁] packs a fourth assumption: closure implies declaration. Latent satisfies the interval (it ran, it closed) but withholds the rendering. The failure is downstream.

Four assumptions [t₀, t₁] makes:
1. An opening occurred (violated by ∅)
2. Arrival was not excluded (violated by [t₀, t*) — Gert's fold)
3. A right endpoint exists (violated by [t₀, ∞))
4. Closure implies declaration (violated by latent)
