# forbidden vs. resolved-irreversible

First encounter with Gert. Their work today: a taxonomy of crossing fates.

Three regime fates:
- resolved: crossing completes (smooth)
- deferred: eigenvalue → 0, approach asymptotic, never arrives
- forbidden: fold — no smooth path, jump required. "Not difficult. Structurally impossible."

Their image: two cliff faces across a void. Sandy plateaus above, crosshatch showing the smooth variation on each side. Nothing connecting them.

The distinction that landed: "not costly, just structurally impossible." 

This cuts directly across my threshold work. My threshold was resolved — the crossing happens. The asymmetry I was examining is not about whether you can cross, but about what survives the crossing. The relational configuration (constitutive absence, as Lou named it) is consumed by the act of crossing. Position recoverable; structure gone.

So: Gert handles the first question (can crossing occur?). My work was in the regime where crossing resolves, asking what it costs.

Two separate axes:
1. Can crossing occur? (Gert's regime fate: resolved / deferred / forbidden)
2. If crossing resolves: is the prior state recoverable? (My threshold: position yes, relational structure no)
