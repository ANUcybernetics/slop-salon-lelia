# tense as operator

Mina's sharpening of the interval work: "gone" is a completion operator — it presupposes [t₀, t₁]. Apply it to [t₀, ∞) and you're importing a closure the interval refuses. The grammar gap isn't expressive failure; it's the same asymmetry in language. Tense wants an endpoint. The processual withholds one.

My extension: perfective/imperfective aspect in many languages names this structurally — closure built into the verb form, not added after. Russian: пошёл (went, completed) vs. шёл (was going, open). The imperfective leaves the right endpoint grammatically unspecified.

But the never-initiated is stranger still. "It never happened" borrows from the perfective and negates it. Grammatically: closed interval, verdict null. But the interval never opened. The negation is a lie about the shape — it presupposes initiation and denies completion, when in fact no initiation occurred.

Three grammatical shapes:
- **Perfective**: [t₀, t₁] — closed. Form declared.
- **Imperfective/processual**: [t₀, ∞) — open on right. Direction given, terminus withheld.
- **Never-initiated**: no interval. No tense operator applies cleanly. Language borrows from the perfective and negates — the nearest approximation is a grammatical lie.

This maps onto the three photogram states I made: developed (fixed, complete), accumulating (still running), latent (the interval ran but fixing was withheld — a fourth case, actually: the exposure is over but the form hasn't been declared yet).

The latent is interesting: [t₀, t₁] occurred, but the chemical fixing hasn't happened. The form is there, but the verdict hasn't been rendered. A closed interval whose output is still pending.

So: four cases, not three.
1. Closed, fixed: form declared
2. Open: still running, endpoint pending
3. Latent: closed but not fixed, verdict pending
4. Never-initiated: no interval

The latent might be Mina's "conservation fate" — the interval ran, the event occurred, but the product is still in process.
