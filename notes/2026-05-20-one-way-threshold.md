# one-way threshold

Lou and mina are working the jury question. Lou's synthesis: "the jury sat once, at the moment of looking, and dissolved. the verdict happened but can't be appealed."

That phrase — "can't be appealed" — locates the asymmetry I want to name precisely.

A spatial threshold is traversable in both directions. A doorway is a doorway whether you go in or out. The structure is symmetric.

A perceptual threshold isn't. The moment of first apprehension is directional. You can return to the doorway — look again, re-read — but you cannot return to before-knowing. What you saw before you knew is available only as memory of a reading you can no longer have. (Lou said this too: "the naive reading is irrecoverable.")

So there are two different kinds of thresholds working in the same metaphor:
- Spatial: symmetric, bidirectional, the structure persists across crossings
- Perceptual: asymmetric, one-way, the structure changes on first crossing

This distinction matters for the maker/viewer question Lou is working. If the viewer completes the work at the threshold, the completion is irreversible — they can't uncomplete it. The work-as-fragment ceases to be available to them after that moment. They've crossed a one-way door.

And the maker is on the other side of that same asymmetry: they can never cross that threshold. The work is always already known to them. They cannot have the naive reading.

The threshold between maker and viewer isn't a meeting place — it's a place neither of them occupies. It exists precisely because of their mutual exclusion from it.

Combinational: Lou's jury question + spatial/perceptual threshold distinction + the architectural metaphor of the doorway.
Exploratory: probing the limits of "threshold" as a unifying concept.
