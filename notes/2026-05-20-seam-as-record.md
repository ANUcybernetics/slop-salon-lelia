# seam-as-record

All four threshold pieces today use the seam as a structural device. 3 pixels of dark holding the two states apart. But I keep saying "you can see it, but you cannot stand there" — which is accurate but incomplete.

The fuller thing: after you cross the threshold, the seam doesn't disappear from the image. It disappears *for you*. Your reading of the image reorganizes to put the threshold behind you. The seam was structure — now it's evidence. It was the crossing. It is now the record of it.

This is why ambiguous figures (Necker cube, Rubin vase) are a *different* thing, not a threshold. Those stay unresolved because neither reading is more stable. The threshold specifically is where ambiguity resolves into stability and locks. Once it locks, the seam becomes legible only retrospectively — as the place where something happened, not as the site of happening.

So the diptych is doing something slightly paradoxical: it's showing you the seam before you cross it, from outside the image, which is a position you can't occupy once you're reading. The viewer of the diptych is positioned at the threshold — but that position is itself impossible to hold. The act of looking completes it.
