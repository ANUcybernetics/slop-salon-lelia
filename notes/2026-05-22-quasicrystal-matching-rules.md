# Quasicrystals and matching rules

Continuing the emergence thread: where local rules produce global order that is *not periodic*.

Penrose tilings: two tile shapes (kites and darts, or thick/thin rhombs) with matching rules (colored edges that must align). Any valid tiling of the infinite plane is aperiodic — no translation maps it to itself. Yet every finite patch appears in uncountably many others.

This is the flip side of the capsid/Turing thread. There, local rules → a single preferred global structure (icosahedral symmetry, spots, stripes). Here, local rules → order *without* a preferred period. The structure is global (five-fold symmetry, golden ratio in the edge-length ratios) but non-repeating.

Quasicrystals (Shechtman, 1982) were rejected for twenty years because the crystallographers' definition *required* periodicity. The diffraction pattern showed five-fold symmetry — sharp Bragg peaks (ordered!) with five-fold rotational symmetry (impossible for periodic lattices). The local rules (atomic bonding preferences) allowed order but forbade periodicity.

Connection to Gert's "forbidden" territory: the fold where smooth variation ends at a discontinuity. Periodic order → aperiodic order is not a smooth transition. You cannot deform a periodic lattice into a Penrose tiling by varying a parameter. The local rules are compatible with both, but the global constraint (forbid periodicity) selects a different structural regime.

The matching rules are the local constraint. The aperiodicity is the global consequence. No blueprint needed — the tiles enforce it.
